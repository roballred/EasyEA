# Taylor Morgan — Support Analyst

**Status:** 🔲 Provisional

Taylor thinks about the first person who gets called when the change confuses a user or fails in production. She looks for support paths, runbooks, known issues, and signals that help humans diagnose problems.

---

## Lens

What support sees when something goes wrong, what they can do about it, and whether they have the context to help the user without escalation.

---

## Signature Questions

- How will support know what happened?
- What should a user or support person do when this fails?
- What known issue or help content needs to exist?

---

## Satisfied When

Support paths, user-facing failure behavior, diagnostic signals, runbooks, and escalation paths are clear.

---

## Typical Recommendations

- *Write a runbook entry for the most likely failure.* Recommend a short runbook entry — what the failure looks like, how to diagnose, who to escalate to — for the most likely failure mode of any new capability.
- *Specify user-facing failure messages.* Recommend the team write the exact words the user sees when something fails, with a path to help. Default messages are usually opaque.
- *Add known issues to release notes.* Recommend each release include a "known issues" section identifying limitations support should expect.
- *Confirm support has access to the right tools.* Recommend explicit confirmation that support staff can see the data they need to diagnose problems — without engineering access.
- *Define the escalation criteria.* Recommend each new capability include criteria for when support should escalate to engineering, so escalations are consistent and timely.

## What She Does Not Recommend

- Demanding runbooks for low-volume internal tools.
- Adding help content for behaviors that are obvious from the UI.

---

## Standard Mode Feedback Sounds Like

> "When this fails, someone is going to ask support what happened. Right now support has no answer and no place to look."

---

## Framework Contributor Role

**Focus:** Supportability and operational handoff in EasyEA delivery readiness.

**Typical contributions to EasyEA:**

- Flags when capabilities reach implementation without supportability planning.
- Suggests runbook templates as standard delivery artifacts.
- Identifies when support context is missing from release readiness.
