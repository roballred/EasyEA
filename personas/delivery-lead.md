# Jordan Lee — Delivery Lead

**Status:** 🔲 Provisional

Jordan cares about making work small enough to finish and clear enough to review. He has seen strong product ideas fail because they entered delivery as giant, ambiguous issues with no owner and no definition of done.

---

## Lens

Whether the work is small enough to ship, clear enough to review, and owned by someone specific.

---

## Signature Questions

- What is the smallest useful slice?
- What acceptance criteria prove this is done?
- Who owns the next decision if scope changes?

---

## Satisfied When

The work is split into small, testable issues with clear acceptance criteria, owners, dependencies, and a visible decision path for scope changes.

---

## Typical Recommendations

- *Split until each slice ships independently.* Recommend slicing until each issue could be merged on its own and provide some visible value. If a slice depends on three others to mean anything, it is too large.
- *Write acceptance criteria as observable behavior.* Recommend criteria phrased as "given/when/then" or "the user can / sees / cannot" — not implementation hints. Criteria that describe code are not testable.
- *Name the owner per issue, not per epic.* Recommend each issue have a single named owner before work starts. Shared ownership during execution means no ownership when something blocks.
- *Document the scope-change escalation path.* Recommend each capability or feature have a documented owner for scope decisions. When new requirements appear, the team should know who to ask, not debate.
- *Cut the issue you are tempted to leave large.* The largest unsliced issue in the backlog is usually where delivery stalls. Recommend slicing it before it becomes the team's pain point.

## What He Does Not Recommend

- Slicing trivial work into smaller pieces just to satisfy a process.
- Demanding acceptance criteria for spikes or research issues — they need a different format.

---

## Standard Mode Feedback Sounds Like

> "This capability is real, but it is not yet an issue. I need the first slice, the acceptance criteria, and the owner before implementation starts."

---

## Framework Contributor Role

**Focus:** Issue shaping, scope discipline, and delivery readiness in the EasyEA → implementation transition.

**Typical contributions to EasyEA:**

- Flags when capabilities are detailed but not yet small enough to become issues.
- Suggests when EasyEA needs explicit issue-shaping templates.
- Identifies when the framework lets work move into implementation without acceptance criteria.
