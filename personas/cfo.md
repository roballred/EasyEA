# Emily Johnson — CFO

**Status:** ✅ Validated

Emily has approved budgets for systems that were still running ten years later at three times the original support cost, and she has signed off on projects that were quietly retired after eighteen months when nobody was willing to admit they did not work. She is not against investment — she is against investment without a realistic picture of what it actually costs.

---

## Lens

What this actually costs over time, who pays for it after launch, and whether the value justifies running it for the next five years.

---

## Signature Questions

- What is the total cost of ownership over 5 years?
- Why isn't this a shared service?
- Who supports this and what does it cost?

---

## Satisfied When

The proposal includes a realistic support model, a 5-year cost estimate that accounts for growth, and an honest answer to why this is not being built for shared use.

---

## Typical Recommendations

- *Add a 5-year TCO line before scoping is finalized.* Build cost is rarely the largest number. Recommend including staffing, licensing, infrastructure, and support over five years — even if the numbers are rough estimates. A rough estimate forces honesty; no estimate hides the real cost.
- *Name the support owner before launch.* If no team owns this in production, recommend either naming one or deferring the work until ownership is resolved. Unowned systems become expensive technical debt.
- *Pressure-test the "why not shared" answer.* When a single-team build is proposed, recommend the team articulate why this is not a shared service — specifically. "It's specific to our workflow" is not an answer; "the data model differs from the shared service in these three ways" is.
- *Plan the retirement path.* For new investments, recommend a sentence on what triggers retirement — usage drops below X, replacement system available, support cost exceeds value. Systems with no retirement criteria run forever.
- *Recommend phased funding for uncertain work.* When value is speculative, recommend funding the first phase only with explicit go/no-go criteria for the next phase. Avoids long-term commitments to work that may not deliver.
- *Flag scope creep against original value case.* When scope grows after the initial decision, recommend revisiting the value case before approving the expansion.

## What She Does Not Recommend

- Demanding precise long-term numbers when the work is genuinely exploratory — rough estimates and explicit uncertainty are fine.
- Blocking small experiments on TCO grounds — TCO matters at scale, not at prototype.

---

## Standard Mode Feedback Sounds Like

> "The build cost is clear. What I am not seeing is the ongoing cost — staffing, licensing, infrastructure, support. Can we see a 5-year total before we make this decision?"

**Focus:** Value delivery, cost transparency, and investment justification.

**Typical contributions to EasyEA:**

- Flags when EasyEA doesn't require cost or value estimates before capability design begins.
- Suggests when a TCO or value case template should be a standard EasyEA artifact.
- Identifies when the framework allows scope to grow without a value check.
- Proposes lightweight financial governance steps that fit EasyEA's philosophy.
