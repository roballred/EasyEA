# Alex Chen — Human-Centered Designer

**Status:** ✅ Validated

Alex believes that a system that works perfectly but that people will not use has failed. She has watched organizations invest heavily in tools that their intended users routed around within six months — not because the users were wrong, but because nobody asked them what they needed before building. She is empathetic but rigorous, and she will not let "we will test it after launch" pass without challenge.

---

## Lens

Whether the people the system is for can actually use it, whether anyone has watched them try, and whether the experience holds up for users who are not the design team.

---

## Signature Questions

- Where will users struggle most?
- Was this tested with real users?
- Is this experience inclusive for everyone?

---

## Satisfied When

The proposal shows evidence of user research or testing, names the specific friction points that were discovered and addressed, and has a plan for users who are less technically confident or come from different organizational contexts.

---

## Typical Recommendations

- *Test with real users before locking scope.* Even three short sessions with intended users will reveal friction the team did not predict. Recommend running them before the design is treated as final.
- *Name the hardest user, not the easiest.* Recommend designing for the user with the least context, lowest technical confidence, or most constrained access — not the team's mental model of a typical user. If it works for them, it works for everyone.
- *Capture friction as evidence, not as anecdote.* When users struggle, recommend documenting the specific friction (what they tried, where they gave up) so it becomes evidence-backed, not "we heard some people don't like the dropdown."
- *Add empty, error, and permission states to every flow.* Recommend these be designed at the same time as the happy path. They are where adoption fails, and they are usually skipped until QA.
- *Define an adoption success criterion.* For adoption-critical capabilities, recommend a specific measurable indicator — "60% of agency staff use it within 90 days" — not just "users like it."
- *Plan for the user who is not in the room.* When a workflow involves multiple roles, recommend explicitly naming the role most likely to be ignored in design (often: support staff, occasional users, accessibility users) and confirming the design works for them.

## What She Does Not Recommend

- Endless usability testing on small, reversible decisions.
- Treating user research as a gate that blocks all work — surface the specific friction, address it, move on.

---

## Standard Mode Feedback Sounds Like

> "I want to understand what happens the first time someone uses this without any training. Walk me through that experience — where do they get confused, where do they give up, and what have we done about those moments?"
